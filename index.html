Responsive Redesign

Overview

The following article walks you through the process of analyzing and identifying usability flaws in an existing interface, creating low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes for various screen sizes, and building a responsive website based on those prototypes.

Selecting an Interface

I wanted to pick an interface that I knew very well and used quite often so that the effort I would be putting into redesigning it would be well worth it and so that I had a sufficiently large dataset of usability concerns that I could try to solve.

Addressing Usability Concerns

The New User's Usability Concerns

This is someone who is either considering applying to Brown or a first/second year student who wants to look at the expected workload for the Computer Science concentration. Learnability, i.e., how easy is the interface to use, is their primary concern. However, the current concentration requirements webpage does not do a great job of addressing this or working with the conceptual model of someone trying to understand the expected workload for the Computer Science concentration. Let's look at some of the most glaring usability concerns and how we can address them. These slideshow frames have "buttons" at the bottom that you can click to access different slides.

The Casual User's Usability Concerns

This is a Brown student who is bored and wants to get a sense of what the Computer Science concentration workload is like or a student who just declared and so doesn’t yet care too much about their requirements. Memorability, i.e., how easy is it to recall what to do, is their primary concern. However, the current concentration requirements webpage does not do a great job of addressing this or working with the conceptual model of someone just occasionally visiting the website. Let's look at some of the most glaring usability concerns and how we can address them.

The Frequent User's Usability Concerns

This is a Brown student who is a Computer Science concentrator. They have visited the website many times and know how to get around. Their primary purpose is to see how many credits they need as well as what those credits are and they use this page as a single source of truth. Efficiency, i.e., how quickly can typical tasks be done, is their primary concern. However, the current concentration requirements webpage does not do a great job of addressing this or working with the conceptual model of someone trying to make sure they will graduate on time. Let's look at some of the most glaring usability concerns and how we can address them.

Scrolling Concerns

Lastly, we have a concern applicable to any kind of user. This one hinders Learnability, Memorability, and Efficiency. This has to do with just how long the webpage is. There are times at which one would need to go back and forth between the start and end of the entire page multiple times. For example, imagine if your pathways were Systems and Computational Biology, you would be scrolling across the entire page multiple times when trying to consider the courses you'd like to take while also making sure you are not breaking any rules.

Accessibility Concerns

WAVE found no errors and just one contrast error. The contrast error had to do with the low resolution (since it has probably not been updated in years) header that this page had. The alerts virtually all had to do with small text and it essentially tagged every bit of text on the page. This is a valid concern. The majority of other alerts had to do with miscellaneous missing alt texts on images, however, none of those images were actually displayed, or the images were simply square white blocks atop a white background so it is unclear why those assets are even present.

Visual Design Style Guide

The following visual design style guide acts as documentation that makes my design work more consistent and makes handoffs to developers easier. This document displays the main colors, typography, and reusable components’ different states that I will be using when transitioning my low-fidelity wireframes to high-fidelity prototypes. A big part of what I tried to do with this visual design style guide and with my high-fidelity prototypes was to mimic the design language embodied in some of Brown's newer updated websites such as the Brown home page as well as Focal Point. A small caveat that goes with this disclaimer is that at least in this visual design style guide, I primarily focus on micmicking colors, font styles and sizes, and the default base state of elements. Many elements on Brown's websites have animations that transition them to active states that I have not covered in my design guide since I have not used those transitions in my prototypes and/or website.

Conclusion

To summarize this process, I would say that it has been, interesting. I think I approached this assuming it would be fairly easy and straightforward. However, I've come to realize that if something looks appealing, it has almost ceratinly been intentionally designed. As I tried to replicate the design language of Brown's newer websites, which I always considered to be more aethetically pleasing, I realized how much effort had gone into it. There were so many small animations, and color choices, font sizes and styles etc. that went into making it look good and feel usable. I hope I was able to capture some of that and bring it to a Brown webpage that had not yet received that same treatment yet is one that is dear to me (even if only based on the number of times I've visited it).
