Responsive Redesign

Overview

The following article walks you through the process of analyzing and identifying usability flaws in an existing interface, creating low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes for various screen sizes, and building a responsive website based on those prototypes.

Selecting an Interface

I wanted to pick an interface that I knew very well and used quite often so that the effort I would be putting into redesigning it would be well worth it and so that I had a sufficiently large dataset of usability concerns that I could try to solve.

The Interface

Brown's Computer Science Conecntration Requirements page!

Addressing Usability Concerns

The New User's Usability Concerns

This is someone who is either considering applying to Brown or a first/second year student who wants to look at the expected workload for the Computer Science concentration. Learnability, i.e., how easy is the interface to use, is their primary concern. However, the current concentration requirements webpage does not do a great job of addressing this or working with the conceptual model of someone trying to understand the expected workload for the Computer Science concentration.

Addressing The New User's Usability Concerns

The Casual User's Usability Concerns

This is a Brown student who is bored and wants to get a sense of what the Computer Science concentration workload is like or a newly declared student who doesn’t yet care too much about requirements. Memorability, i.e., how easy is it to recall what to do, is their primary concern. However, the current concentration requirements webpage does not do a great job of addressing this or working with the conceptual model of someone just occasionally visiting the website.

Addressing The Casual User's Usability Concerns

The Frequent User's Usability Concerns

This is a Brown student who is a declared Computer Science concentrator. They have visited the website many times and know how to get around. Their primary purpose is to see both how many credits they need as well as what those credits can be and they will use this as a single source of truth. However, the current concentration requirements webpage does not do a great job of addressing this or working with the conceptual model of someone trying to make sure they will graduate on time.

Addressing The Frequent User's Usability Concerns

Accessibility Concerns

WAVE found no errors and just one contrast error. The contrast error had to do with the low resolution (and probably not been updated in years) header that this page had. The alerts virtually all had to do with small text and it essentially tagged every bit of text on the page. This is a valid concern. The majority of other alerts had to do with miscellaneous missing alt texts on images, however, none of those images were actually displayed, or the images were simply square white blocks atop a white background so it is unclear why those assets are even present.

Personas

These personas are based on overarching trends I observed amongst all the users I interviewed, not characteristics I observed from a specific individual. I've created a four-quadrant empathy map, describing what users think, feel, say, and do for one archetypal character that will represent part of the spectrum of users I observed.

Persona 1: Introverted Izzy

Izzy is an introvert and likes to avoid unnecessary human interaction when possible. They normally go up to the counter but get nervous and feel awkward about wasting the cashier’s time and order the standard drink which they don’t actually enjoy. They are reluctant to ask for help when they are not able to figure something out and get flustered very easily. So, they are very happy about being able to use an automated kiosk to order instead of doing so at the counter and take their own sweet time while feeling very comfortable.

Persona 2: Frustrated Frank

Frank is short tempered and not having a good day. Already frustrated, they come to the cafe for a drink to cool off; but every part of the process seems to not work as it should, frustrating them even further. They just want to get out of there as fast as they can but they are not able to. They don't read any of the instructions and try to speed through the process while also pressing harder than they should on the touchscreen causing glitches. They are unhappy about the bilingual nature of the menu and the resulting lack of clarity on certain menu items. The lack of explanation on the rewards process and the fact that they are spending so much time on this all contributes to an unpleasant experience.

Conclusion

To summarize this process, I think it's clear that personas and storyboards are powerful tools that help capture the intricaies and nuances of a user experience in a way that simpler tools like user roles, market segments etc. simply cannot do. Automation in industries like food are often touted as an unnecessary evil and articles like this one explain why it could be, as it was for Frustrated Frank, but hopefully Introverted Izzy was a good example of why it could be a blessing to some as well.
